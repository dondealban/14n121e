UNDERSTANDING THE PROXIMATE AND UNDERLYING DRIVERS OF DEFORESTATION AND LAND USE CHANGE IN MYANMAR (2016–2019).

The project aims to understand the myriad proximate and underlying drivers of deforestation and land use/cover change in Myanmar, which is a country that is currently undergoing political and economic changes. This dual political and economic transtion offers opportunities for economic growth and development but may put increasing pressure on its forests. The various research strands that I am pursuing in this project include, but are not limited to: investigating net and gross land cover changes using global time-series land cover map products, as well as a combination of multi-temporal and multi-sensor satellite imagery to better discriminate between land use/cover types, and subsequently analyse land cover changes; analysing the spatial landscape patterns and spatial determinants of land cover change, specifically the conversion of forests into agricultural plantations such as oil palm and rubber; and simulating future forest cover change with the aim of developing scenarios of land use policy options that may serve to shape the future agricultural and conservation landscapes of Myanmar. I am also collaborating with other members of our research team on topics such as investigating the proliferation of land acquisitions across Southeast Asia (with a special future interest in applying the telecoupling framework); and understanding the both formal and informal gold mining industry in Myanmar using household surveys and field interviews, statistical analyses, and agent-based modeling.
